<div style="display: none"><pre></pre></div>
      <span class="print-link"><span class="print_html"><a href="https://www.healthgrain.org/print/1" title="Display a printer-friendly version of this page." class="print-page" onclick="window.open(this.href); return false" rel="nofollow"><img src="/sites/all/modules/print/icons/print_icon.gif" alt="Printer-friendly version" title="Printer-friendly version" width="16" height="16" class="print-icon" /></a></span></span><h2 style="text-align: justify;">Healthgrain Forum Session at the FENS 2015 Conference, Berlin, 20 October 2015</h2><p style="text-align: justify;"><img style="float: left; border: 1px solid black; margin-left: 0px; margin-right: 12px;" title="HGF Session at FENS2015" src="/sites/healthgrain.org/files/img/thumbnail_FENS2015_HGF_Session_Flyer_80x113.png" alt="HGF Session at FENS2015" width="80" height="113" />The presentations from the highly appreciated and well-attended Healthgrain Forum Session at the FENS 2015 conference 20 October 2015 are available for public download at <a href="/events/fens2015" target="_blank">www.healthgrain.org/events/fens2015</a>. Based on the recent presentation <a href="/webfm_send/858" target="_blank">Environmental impact of bread and breakfast cereals</a>, Prof. Riccardi also paid attention to sustainability, showing the excellent eco-efficiency of (whole-)grain-based products.</p><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</p><h2 style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">New publication: "Nutritional Impacts of Different Whole Grain Milling Techniques: A Review of Milling Practices and Existing Data"</h2><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">Julie Miller Jones, Judi Adams, Cynthia Harriman, Chris Miller, and Jan Willem Van der Kamp<br />Cereal Foods World May/June 2015, Volume 60, Number 3 Pages 130-139 <a href="http://cerealchemistry.aaccnet.org/doi/abs/10.1094/CFW-60-3-0130" target="_blank">http://cerealchemistry.aaccnet.org/doi/abs/10.1094/CFW-60-3-0130</a><br />Key items in this comprehensive article include an overview of the history of milling and the impact of different milling processes on nutrients. Main conclusion: Data comparing 'classical' single-stream stone-grinding milling and the currently predominantly used multiple-stream milling with recombination do not show any strong advantage for either milling method.</p><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">EUFIC updated review on whole grains</h2><p>The EUFIC updated review on whole grains is now online at <a href="http://www.eufic.org/article/en/page/RARCHIVE/expid/whole_grains_2015/">http://www.eufic.org/article/en/page/RARCHIVE/expid/whole_grains_2015/</a></p><p>&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">Whole Grain Summit 2015 in Portland, OR.</h2><p>All Presentations have been posted to the Moore Family Center website at <a href="http://health.oregonstate.edu/moore-center/whole-grain-summit" target="_blank">http://health.oregonstate.edu/moore-center/whole-grain-summit</a>.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">Join the global research initiative to unravel wheat and gluten related health concerns</h2><p style="text-align: justify;">Based on a number of recent studies, the related questions about the level of evidence and a very strong social media drive, to avoid the consumption of gluten-containing grains, there is an urgent need for well controlled studies. The latter is especially the case since in most published studies the types of grains used and the foods produced from them have been very poorly characterized and described, making many conclusions questionable. Future studies should address the detailed composition of different cereals used, the changes in composition that may occur as a result of food processing (dough making using yeast or sourdough culture, extrusion, time, temperature, etc.) as well as final baking, drying, etc.</p><p style="text-align: justify;">The ultimate aim of such an approach is to have a full analytical picture of the actual food that will be supplied to individuals for the study of effects on metabolism, symptoms and well-being. When addressing the in vivo effects of wheat based foods, consumed "as part of a typical daily diet", only a known composition will help to obtain reliable data. The latter is of crucial importance to make evidence based recommendations for future product development in the context of "good food for a healthy life". Here we propose an international project of global importance to the cereal supply chain and food industry.</p><p style="text-align: justify;">The HealthGrain Forum Europe, endorsed by <a href="https://www.icc.or.at/" target="_blank">ICC</a> and <a href="http://www.aaccnet.org/" target="_blank">AACC</a>, is <strong>seeking for active participation of the cereal foods chain related academic and industrial partners</strong>. Read the project <a href="https://www.icc.or.at/sites/icc.or.at/files/file_upload/page/2577/1_Project_addressing_wheat_avoidance_-_Presentation.pdf" target="_blank">Presentation</a> and&nbsp;<a href="https://www.icc.or.at/sites/icc.or.at/files/file_upload/page/2577/2_Project_addressing_wheat_avoidance_-_Description.pdf" target="_blank">Description</a> for more information about the backgrounds and aims of this pre-competitive research project.</p><p style="text-align: justify;">For expression of interest and full project details contact Fred Brouns from the HealthGrain Forum at <span class="spamspan"><span class="u">fred [dot] brouns</span> [at] <span class="d">maastrichtuniversity [dot] nl</span></span>.</p><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">HealthBread EU Project – successful market introductions of healthy breads based on HEALTHGRAIN principles</h2><p style="text-align: justify;">A project consortium led by TNO, with other HGForum members and eight small and medium sized bakeries in Austria, Germany, Italy and the Netherlands successfully executed the EU project HealthBread - product innovation based on FP6 HEALTHGRAIN results and knowledge (2012-2014). By applying advanced milling technologies and long fermentation processes ("bioprocessing") HealthBread bakeries introduced as top products of their range clean label breads with high levels of fibre and micronutrients, with an attractive taste. HealthBread coordinator Jan Willem van der Kamp was elected by the Bakery and Snacks Journal as Bakery personality of the year 2014. See also <a href="http://ec.europa.eu/research/bioeconomy/news-events/news/20150130_en.htm" target="_blank">ec.europa.eu/research/bioeconomy/news-events/news/20150130_en.htm</a> and <a href="http://www.healthbread.eu/" target="_blank">www.healthbread.eu</a>.</p><p style="text-align: justify;"><a title="Enlarge image" href="/sites/healthgrain.org/files/img/2015/HealthBread_van-der-Kamp_Becker_515x262.jpg" target="_blank"><img style="float: left; border: 1px solid black; margin-top: 0px; margin-bottom: 10px; margin-left: 0px; margin-right: 10px;" src="/sites/healthgrain.org/files/img/2015/HealthBread_van-der-Kamp_Becker_515x262.jpg" alt="" width="250" /></a></p><p style="text-align: justify;">Project Coordinator Drs. Jan Willem van der Kamp presents the HealthBread <em>Bakers Manual</em> (4 versions: Dutch, English, German, Italian to Mr. Peter Becker – President of the German Association of Artisan Bakeries and President of the International Union of Bakeries and Confectioneries (UIBC).</p><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">New presentation on Health Effects of Cereal Fibre!</h2><p style="text-align: justify;"><img style="float: left; margin-left: 0px; margin-right: 12px;" title="HGF presentation: Health Effects of Cereal Fibre" src="/sites/healthgrain.org/files/img/thumbnail_HGF-presentation_Health_Effects_of_Cereal_Fibre_151x113.png" alt="HGF presentation: Health Effects of Cereal Fibre" width="151" height="113" />The Healthgrain Forum has prepared a presentation entitled Health Effects of Cereal Fibre. The presentation provides a thorough introduction to the legislative field of cereal fibre, analytical methods used in practice, and informs about the fibre contents in different grains and fractions. Furthermore, the presentation sets this in the context of the present and future health challenges Europe is facing; explains the health effects of cereal fibre with reference to systematic reviews and meta-analyses; and highlights finally different national recommendations for daily intake of cereal fibre while also providing recommendations to consumers on how to increase their daily intake of cereal fibre. The presentations is available as pdf-version for free download here:</p><p style="text-align: justify;"><a href="/webfm_send/766" target="_blank"><strong>HEALTHGRAIN Forum Presentation "Health Effects of Cereal Fibre"</strong></a> (Jan. 2015)</p><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">New PhD thesis by Juhani Sibakov: Processing of oat dietary fibre for improved functionality as a food ingredient</h2><p style="text-align: justify;"><img style="float: left; border: 1px solid black; margin-left: 0px; margin-right: 12px;" title="Juhani Sibakov" src="/sites/healthgrain.org/files/pictures/picture-113.jpg" alt="Juhani Sibakov" width="100" />VTT's Research Scientist <a href="/juhani_sibakov" target="_blank">Juhani Sibakov</a> developed in his PhD work oat fractions, which contained up to 56% beta-glucan or 73% protein. These are the highest contents ever reached by a method without any water or solvents. In the beginning of the process, oats were defatted by supercritical carbon dioxide extraction. From the defatted oats, it was then possible to separate highly enriched beta-glucan fractions. A beta-glucan content of approximately 35% was achieved using only dry grinding and air classification. This kind of beta-glucan fraction could be an interesting ingredient for the food processing industry because its yield was approximately 8-10% from the oat raw material. The separation process developed at VTT was scaled up to industrial scale in Germany, and it is expected to be commercialised within the next few years. The applicability of beta-glucan rich fractions was studied in beverages and extruded snacks.</p><p style="text-align: justify;">Read the <a href="http://www.vtt.fi/news/2014/Healthy_fractions_of_oats_Sibakov.jsp?lang=en" target="_blank">entire press release</a>.</p><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">New PhD thesis by Outi Santala: Impact of water content on enzymatic modification of wheat bran</h2><p style="text-align: justify;"><img style="float: left; border: 1px solid black; margin-left: 0px; margin-right: 12px;" title="Outi Santala" src="/sites/healthgrain.org/files/img/Outi_Santala_100x147.jpg" alt="Outi Santala" width="100" height="147" />VTT's Research Scientist Outi Santala showed in her doctoral dissertation that technological functionality of wheat bran can be improved by enzymatic modification at a low water content of 40–50%. The results of the thesis can be utilized for improving the technological functionality of bran in food applications and for developing new processes for the enzymatic modification of plant raw materials at reduced water content.</p><p style="text-align: justify;">Read the <a href="http://www.vtt.fi/news/2014/topicals/04062014_vaitos_vesipitoisuuden_vaikutus_vehnaleseen_entsymaattisessa_muokkauksessa.jsp?lang=en" target="_blank">entire press release</a>.</p><p style="clear: both; text-align: justify;">&nbsp;</p><h2 style="text-align: justify;">HEALTHGRAIN whole grain definition - Now published!</h2><p style="text-align: justify;"><em>Van der Kamp J.W., Poutanen K, Seal C, Richardson D</em>: <strong>The HEALTHGRAIN definition of "whole grain"</strong> has been published in Food and Nutrition Research, the on-line peer reviewed Journal where more whole grain definition related papers have been published.
